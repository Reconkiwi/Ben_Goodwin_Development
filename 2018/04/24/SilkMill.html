The Silk Mill - Y2S2

The Brief

For our client projects this semester, we were given a series of briefs to complete in groups. My group and I opted to take on Whitchurch Silk Mill’s brief which was to develop a way of tracking and evaluating the visitors that entered the mill as well as monitor the popularity of their new exhibits.
Whitchurch Silk Mill had recently been given an investment from the Heritage Lottery Fund, and as part of their investment, they were asked to produce software that can process data as evidence of how their funding is improving the accessibility, popularity and maintenance of the silk mill.
Throughout the duration of this project, Zoe was our point of contact. Although Zoe was very open to a variety of different ways in which we could develop this project, she did have a few specifications for us to consider.

Zoe’s Specifications

  • Informative
  • Consistent design
  • Whitchurch Silk Mill branding
  • Look and feel eye-catching
  • Simple to use
Even with the specifications that Zoe had, Jack and I had a lot of freedom to develop something unique and exciting for Whitchurch Silk Mill. From the brief, it was clear that to complete this task to a high standard we needed to take a user first approach and therefore, features such as navigation and accessibility were extremely important.

However, after some discussion and brainstorming, we felt that we could both manage the workload as long as we stayed in contact and updated each other on our progress throughout the semester.

Initial Brainstorming and Ideas

We began our project by brainstorming ideas and ways in which we would tackle the project in a systematic manner. During this period, we tried to breakdown the project into a series of smaller tasks that would need to be completed. We managed to seperate our project into three main tasks

The Breakdown

  • Voulunteer/Evaluation system
  • Databases and Backend systems
  • User operated system
Having broken the project down, it was clear that we needed to create a product that took user input in an imaginative way, recorded it, sent it to a database where the information would be stored and then, finaly, retrieved from the database and presented in a visually pleasing manner for the silk mill staff to show the Heritage Lottery Fund.
To understand ways in which we could accomplish this, we mocked up a series of ideas.

Idea 1 - Facebook Detectron

From an article we had read about the Facebook Detectron system, a fast flexible object detection system built on Caffe2, we considered developing a camera system that took images of Silk Mill attendees througout a day and ,through the use of detectron, the silk mill voulenteers would be able to find out the number of attendees that came on specific days as well as the exhibits they visited. The photos would then be deleted to save space on the system.

Problems With Idea 1

Although we felt that using the detectron system would be a very unique way for Whitchurch Silk Mill to evaluate the people that visited, it was clear that the development for this project may be very challenging and time consuming. As well as this the idea didnt offer visitors the chance to evaluate their experience which was a specification set by the mill.

Idea 2 - RFID

Our second idea was to develop an RFID system where, upon entry, visitors would be given an RFID card with predetermined information on it such as age range or gender. As visitors walked around the silk mill, they would be able to scan their cards and begin interactive experiences or restart videos that were already playing. Each scan on their card would send data to a database that collected information about the various exhibits they had been to as well as the predefined information already on the card.

Problems With Idea 2

Although we felt that this system would work at the silk mill, it was clear that the information we would be collecting was not specific enough for the voulenteers at Whitchurch Silk Mill to find enough supporting evidence to give to the Heritage Lottery Fund. As well as this, we decided that it would be difficult to precisely predefine information on an RFID card for each person that visited.

Idea 3 - Mobile App

Our third idea was to develop a mobile virtual tour guide application where visitors would be given tablets on entry with the virtual guides installed on them. Visitors would be asked at the beginning of the application to fill out a short survey to help the silk mill to gather data. Having done this, visitors would then gain access to exclusive virtual content about each exhibition. by clicking each exhibition tile on the application, information would be sent to a database where silk mill voulenteers would be able to see the amount of times each exhibit was attended per visitor.
They would then be able to notice patterns in exhibition visits based on visitor age, gender and nationality which they could send to the Heritage Lottey Fund

Problems With Idea 3

One of the key issues with this proposed idea was that we had planned to develop our own Rasberry Pi tablets, programmed to only access the web application. Theoretically, this would have been a really interesting way to implement our web application, however, the system would only work if we had access to multiple programmed Rasberry Pi tablets rather than just one. This is because Whitchurch Silk Mill has a multitude of visitors throughout a day (24,000 a year) and would likely require multiple tablets to satisfy their capacity.
As well as this the development costs of creating Rasberry Pi touchscreens would cost around Â£120 for each device. This outcome would be very costly for us as well as the client.

Presentation

As the mobile application project (idea 3) was far more feasable than the other two options, we agreed with the client that we would develop this system. Having said this, after our discussion with the client, it was clear that developing the Rasberry Pi mobile devices was unfeasable. Instead of this we decided to develop our application as a web app hosted on a private network that would be accessible to any silk mill visitors with a SMART phone. We felt that this was a good remedy for the situation and also made the system more accessible for visitors.

As well as this the application files would take up around 350KB of space. Initial Development

Database

I began development by creating a database for our data to be collected in. During the first week, Jack worked alongside me to ensure that we both understood how the database would work and so that we both had a copy of the same table setup (Same names and types).
From our research we had found that we needed to set our ID row to ‘Primary’ as well as ‘AUTO_INCREMENT’, therefore, we implemented this into our database. ‘AUTO_INCREMENT’ is by default set to 1 and will increment by 1 for each new record. This is useful for the ID row as each visitor that is recorded in the database needs a unique ID. The Primary key is used to uniquely define a record. As there can only be one primary key in a table, it should be set to the most unique set of data within a record e.g. the ID so that each visitor can be defined.
We then created a TIMESTAMP for each record so that the silk mill can see the times in which different visitors attended the silk mill exhibitions.
Finally, we created the rest of the datasets that the silk mill needed to gather such as age, gender, country and exhibitions. For the prototype we limited the number of different exhibitions to 6 so that we could create a far fully fleshed out prototype.

Evaluation System - Version 1

Once Jack had mocked up a simple way of updating the database with information (through the virtual tour guide), it was my turn to begind developing a system that turned that data into graphs and charts.
To draw data from the database, we required a PHP document that connected our application to the MySQL database. This is then called at the start of each page of the application.
Having set up a connection to the database I then had to develop a way of pulling sets of data to implement into my Chart.JS charts. I began by creating a MySQL query that pulled all data from the ‘users’ database.
To set the data that I wanted to pull from the database I created an array of the rows that I needed. If the array is equal to the result, the query fetches the data from the database.
The result of this displays a string of data for each unique user. With the data pulled from the database, I was then able to call data.PHP in my Chart.JS page
I began my development by following a series of tutorials explaining how to import MySQL data into Chart.JS. Although, I quickly managed to create a very simple chart, I found it difficult to process and present the right data. Learning Chart,JS was not necessarily the problem, it was more that my MySQL queries were not sending the right information. I began troubleshooting my data.php file and messed around with my queries until I was able to represent the data that I wanted onto a graph.
I set the label dataset of the X-axis to take age from data.php. By doing this I was able to see each individual record across my X-axis with each exxhibit dataset along the Y-axis. However, the age dataset did not group people of similar ages. I experimented with my MySQL query and added ‘GROUP BY age’ to the end of this. This made visitors with the exact same age grouped onto my graph which was a slight improvement from what it previously was.
Unfortunately, I still felt as if the chart was not up to standard. I tried creating a MySQL query that set age ranges, however, I could not get the query to work. It wasn’t until the last version of the evaluation system that I was able to define age groups rather than individual ages.
After many failed attempts to improve the quality of my chart data, I decided to focus my work on developing another, more basic, chart ready for our first evaluation. I attempted to develop a doughnut chart that represented the total number of times each exhibit had been visited.

Safeguarding Information

The final piece of feedback I was given was to ensure that the evaluation software was protected so that only volunteers at the silk mill could access the data collected.
To do this, I created a basic password Javascript script that required a user to login to access the evaluation software. Although this system worked, I had noticed that anyone could find the password in the script through the inspect function on a webpage. This meant that the password was not secure at all. I decided to try and find a way of encrypting the password so that it would not be visible inside of the inspector. I began researching into htaccess and php login pages where passwords would be stored inside of databases.
The problem with this was that, I would have to backtrack my work and develop an entirely new login system which I felt would set my progress back. Eventually, I came across another way of hiding my password. The solution to my problem was to obfuscate my script. This method jumbled my JS code into a more obscure format. Although, this is not the most complex safeguarding system, I believed that this was complex enough for the prototype we were developing. I feel that this was an important addition to my project as data security is extremely important in today’s society.

Changes, Fixes and Development for Final Product

Having considered the feedback during the week 10 crit revue. It was clear that I had to re-evaluate my safeguarding system. I began by looking at ways of redirecting my page unless a user had logged in.
I ended up developing a system using PHP where, through the use of sessions and defining ‘current’ and ‘last page’, I was able to check whether or not a use has accessed the page before. After this an ‘if’ statement is called that redirects the user from the evaluation page if they have not successfully logged in through the login page
I was really happy with how well this system worked. No matter how i tried to gain access to the page I would always be redirected back to the login page. I think that this really strengthened the safeguarding system I had in place. With this system now in place, and more time left to experiment with the project, I decided to have a look at other areas I could strengthen.
